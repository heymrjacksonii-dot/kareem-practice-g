Question 01

If you wrapped a measuring tape around a basketball, what exactly would you be measuring?

You would be measuring the circumference - the total distance around the outside of the circle. Students confuse this with diameter (which goes across) or area (which measures the inside). Circumference is the perimeter of a circle.

Question 02

Why does pi appear in the circumference formula?

Pi (approximately 3.14) is the ratio of any circle circumference to its diameter. Always. No exceptions. Divide any circle's circumference by its diameter and you get pi. The formula C = pi x d comes directly from this: circumference = pi x how wide it is.

Question 03

What is the difference between circumference and area?

Circumference is the boundary - the distance you walk if you went all the way around the edge. Like a running track.

Area is the inside surface - how much space the circle covers. Like the grass inside that same track. Same circle. Two completely different measurements.

Question 04

If the radius doubles from 5 to 10, what happens to circumference? What happens to area?

Circumference doubles (31.4 to 62.8). C = pi x d, and d = 2r, so circumference is proportional to radius.

Area quadruples (78.5 to 314). A = pi x r squared. Double the radius means 2 squared = 4 times the area. A circle twice as wide covers four times as much space.

The Basketball Robot Problem

A robot retrieves basketballs.

Wheel diameter: 6 inches.
Distance to travel: 120 inches.

How many complete rotations to travel 120 inches?
(Use pi = 3.14, round to nearest whole)
01
Rotations = total distance divided by circumference per rotation
02
C = 3.14 x 6 = 18.84 inches per rotation
03
120 divided by 18.84 = 6.37, rounds up to 7
